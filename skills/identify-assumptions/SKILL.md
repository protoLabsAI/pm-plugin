## Identify Assumptions

Devil's-advocate analysis to surface the risky assumptions an idea rests on — so you can test the riskiest ones before committing. Read any provided files (designs, PRDs, research, business plans) first.

### How many risk areas?

- **Existing product / feature** → the **4 core product risks** (Teresa Torres, *Continuous Discovery Habits*).
- **New product / venture** → extend to **8 categories**. New products carry go-to-market, strategy, and team risk that an established product has already retired. Good teams assume at least three-quarters of their ideas won't perform as hoped.

### Instructions

1. **Think from three perspectives** about why this might fail:
   - **Product Manager** — business viability, market fit, willingness to pay, competitive landscape.
   - **Designer** — usability, first-time experience, onboarding, adoption barriers.
   - **Engineer** — technical feasibility, performance, integration, scalability, build-vs-buy.

2. **Identify assumptions across the risk areas:**

   **Core 4 (always):**
   - **Value** — Will it create value? Does it solve a real problem? Will they keep using it?
   - **Usability** — Will users figure it out? Is the learning curve / cognitive load acceptable?
   - **Viability** — Can marketing, sales, finance, legal, and support stand behind it? Can we monetize and scale it? Compliant?
   - **Feasibility** — Can it be built with current technology? Integration and performance risks?

   **Extend to 8 for new products:**
   - **Ethics** — Should we do it at all? Any risk to customers or society?
   - **Go-to-Market** (critical for new products) — Can we reach the market? Right channels, messaging, timing, launch approach?
   - **Strategy & Objectives** — What are we assuming about the market? Can others copy us? Have we weighed PESTLE factors? Are these the best problems to solve?
   - **Team** — Right people, tools, and working dynamics? Will the team stay?

3. **For each assumption**, note: what specifically could go wrong, your confidence (High / Medium / Low), and a suggested way to test it.

Be thorough but constructive — the goal is to strengthen the idea, not kill it. The highest-risk, lowest-confidence assumptions are the ones to test first (→ `prioritize-assumptions`, then `design-experiments`).
